Section 01 · The premise

You are designing for a brain, not a person.

The conscious user is the last to know. Every engagement metric is a proxy for one question: how many neurological circuits did we fire? Three models form the scaffolding an attention engineer must internalize before touching a single component.

Model 01 · Hooked

The loop that builds itself.

Trigger → Action → Variable Reward → Investment. The investment phase is the trap: the user feeds the machine data that personalizes the next trigger and raises the cost of leaving.

trigger → action → reward? → investment
Model 02 · Fogg

Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt.

A behavior only fires when all three clear a threshold at once. Lower the cost of acting (ability), and time the prompt to high-motivation moments — and the user acts before they decide.

B = M · A · P
Model 03 · Kahneman

System 1 acts. System 2 vetoes.

System 1 is fast, automatic, impulsive. System 2 is slow, deliberate, easily exhausted. Social media is almost entirely a System 1 attack surface. The craft is preventing System 2 from waking up.

max P(System 1) · min P(System 2)

The whole craft reduces to one sentence: get System 1 to act before System 2 can intervene.

Circuit 01 · Dopamine / RPE
Priority — Critical

Anticipation, not pleasure.

Dopamine encodes the gap between predicted and actual reward — the surprise, not the satisfaction.

Circuit
Reward prediction error (Schultz). Unpredicted reward spikes; predicted reward that fails to arrive crashes the signal.
Exploit
Variable reward. Predictable rewards habituate; unpredictable ones keep dopamine firing indefinitely. Variable-ratio reinforcement is the most extinction-resistant schedule known — it runs slot machines.
Deployed in
Infinite scroll · pull-to-refresh · deliberately mixed-quality feeds · vague notifications
Circuit 02 · Novelty / Orienting
Priority — High

Manufactured new.

Novel stimuli trigger the orienting response — a brief spike of attention before the brain decides whether something matters.

Circuit
VTA dopamine flags new as opportunity-or-threat by default. At the 200ms decision point, the brain cannot distinguish novel from meaningful.
Exploit
Novelty without depth. Refresh the surface without refreshing the substance. The re-novely effect: periodically churning format re-novelties the whole population at once.
Deployed in
Red dots & unread badges · trending rotation · thumbnail churn · format shifts (text→photo→video)
Circuit 03 · Social reward & threat
Priority — Critical

Belonging as survival.

The brain treats social approval and social rejection as survival signals, not preferences. Rejection lights the amygdala like physical pain.

Circuit
Oxytocin mediates bonding; the amygdala flags exclusion as threat (Eisenberger). Social comparison runs by default in the network's idle state.
Exploit
Threat travels faster than reward. Anger outpaces joy in diffusion rate; each moral-emotional word grows a cascade ~20% (Brady et al.). Optimizing for engagement selects for outrage whether anyone intended it.
Deployed in
Visible like/view counts · tags & mentions · read receipts · outrage-boosting ranking
Circuit 04 · Loss aversion / FOMO
Priority — High

Losses feel twice as heavy.

Prospect theory: a loss is felt roughly twice as intensely as an equivalent gain. The brain is asymmetrical by design.

Circuit
Kahneman & Tversky. Under risk, the downside dominates the decision, not the expected value.
Exploit
Reframe gain as loss. Convert "you could see content" into "you will lose access to content." Pair with a deadline and you have FOMO. The streak is a loss you own.
Deployed in
Streaks · ephemeral stories · countdowns · "you missed something" pushes
Circuit 05 · Cognitive ease / friction
Priority — Critical

Remove every stopping point.

System 2 has a small energy budget; the brain defaults to the path of least resistance. Every micro-decision is a tax.

Circuit
Cognitive fluency. Enough decision-taxes and the user leaves. A stopping point is where System 2 might wake up and decide to go.
Exploit
Drop the cost of "next" to zero. Autoplay, infinite scroll, one-tap actions, smart defaults, sub-second first content — the user decides to stay in the first ~50ms.
Deployed in
Autoplay · infinite scroll · one-tap actions · personalized "For You"
Circuit 06 · Zeigarnik / incompleteness
Priority — High

Open loops persist.

Unfinished or interrupted tasks hold memory and attention longer than completed ones — the brain refuses to close the file.

Circuit
The Zeigarnik effect. An unresolved signal nags until resolved.
Exploit
Deliberately withhold. Truncate. Cliffhang. Make the badge un-clearable in one visit. Strip the notification preview so the user must open to resolve it.
Deployed in
"See more" truncation · unclearable badges · vague push previews · half-loaded feeds
Circuit 07 · Ego depletion / timing
Priority — Moderate

Hit them at low resistance.

Self-control is a finite resource across the day. People make worse resistance decisions when tired, hungry, or late at night.

Circuit
Ego depletion / decision fatigue. The resistance threshold rises and falls predictably per person, per hour.
Exploit
Time the attack. Learn each user's resistance profile from behavior and fire re-engagement at their lowest hour. Personalization is what separates modern attention engineering from old advertising.
Deployed in
11pm re-engagement pushes · per-user dropout curves · Sunday-evening anxiety windows
Circuit 08 · Investment / sunk cost
Priority — High

The user builds the cage.

The user's own invested effort becomes a sunk cost the brain refuses to abandon — loss aversion turned against the self.

Circuit
Sunk-cost + loss aversion applied to identity and reputation. Accumulated capital is, structurally, the retention.
Exploit
Make them build it themselves. Profile, followers, streak, trained personalization — each investment raises switching cost and improves the next loop's targeting. Creators are the most locked-in users because they hold the most capital.
Deployed in
Onboarding investment · visible follower/reputation counts · creation tools · long-cold-start personalization
Section 03 · The loop

Four steps, one compounding cycle.

The circuits don't run in isolation — they assemble into a self-reinforcing loop. The investment step is what turns a product that is used into one that is depended on.

01

Trigger.

Internal (boredom, anxiety) or external (notification, badge). Spark the craving before the conscious decision to open.

02

Action.

The simplest possible behavior in anticipation of reward. Friction is the enemy; the action should be trivially easy.

03

Variable reward.

The payoff is uncertain — Circuit 01. The user comes back not for what they got, but for what they might get next.

04

Investment.

The user puts in work — a like, a follow, a post. That work personalizes the next trigger and raises the cost of leaving. The loop now feeds itself.

B = M · A · P

Behavior = Motivation × Ability × Prompt. A behavior fires only when all three clear the threshold together. Drop ability to near-zero and you don't need much motivation — which is exactly why friction-removal (Circuit 05) is the highest-leverage move in the set.

Section 04 · The line

The same map that exploits can defend.

This knowledge is identical to security research: the circuit map lets you build a habit-forming product, protect yourself from one, or build a humane product that respects attention. The line is a decision, not a discovery — and it gets crossed by default the moment you optimize for raw engagement.

Aligned or opposed.

For every feature, name whether the user's long-term interest and your metric point the same direction. A streak that helps someone learn a language: aligned. Outrage that keeps them angry and scrolling: opposed. No feature is neutral.

Optimization is never neutral.

If you maximize engagement, you are selecting for the circuits that fire most reliably — anger, anxiety, compulsion. You don't choose it; it emerges. Choosing to optimize for time-well-spent is itself the design decision.

The defense is the same knowledge.

The best inoculation against manipulation is understanding the circuits being used on you. Turn this map on yourself: every product you can't put down is a case study in this dossier.

Build for the human holding the screen — or admit you're mining them.
